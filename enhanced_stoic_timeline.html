Socrates

c. 470-399 BCE
Influence

The foundational figure whose teachings on virtue and the examined life profoundly influenced all subsequent philosophical schools, including Stoicism.

Plato

c. 428-348 BCE
Academy

Student of Socrates who founded the Academy. His theory of Forms and virtue ethics influenced Stoic philosophy.

D

Diogenes of Sinope

c. 412-323 BCE
Cynic

Founder of Cynicism whose philosophy of living according to nature directly influenced early Stoicism.

Learn more →
Ar

Aristotle

384-322 BCE
Logic

Student of Plato who developed systematic logic and ethics, significantly influencing Stoic philosophy.

Learn more →
E

Epicurus

c. 341-270 BCE
Rival

Founder of Epicureanism, the main philosophical rival to Stoicism, advocating pleasure as the highest good.

Learn more →
Z

Zeno of Citium

c. 334-262 BCE
Founder

Founder of Stoicism who established the school in Athens around 300 BCE. Combined Cynic ethics with academic philosophy to create systematic Stoic doctrine.

Learn more →
C

Cleanthes

c. 331-232 BCE
2nd Head

Second head of the Stoa who developed Stoic physics and theology. Known for his devotional approach to philosophy and poetic writings.

Learn more →
Ch

Chrysippus

c. 280-207 BCE
3rd Head

Third head of the Stoa, considered the "second founder" of Stoicism. Systematized Stoic doctrine and wrote over 700 works defending Stoic philosophy.

Learn more →

Middle Stoa

150-55 BCE • Roman Integration & Adaptation
DB

Diogenes of Babylon

c. 240-152 BCE
Head

Stoic philosopher who led the famous Athenian embassy to Rome in 155 BCE, introducing Stoicism to Roman culture.

Learn more →
A

Antipater of Tarsus

2nd century BCE
Head

Head of the Stoa who bridged Early and Middle Stoicism, developing more nuanced views on emotions.

Learn more →
P

Panaetius

c. 185-109 BCE
Head

Leader of Middle Stoicism who adapted Stoic doctrine for Roman culture and influenced Cicero.

Learn more →
Po

Posidonius

135-51 BCE
Polymath

Brilliant polymath who integrated scientific research with Stoic philosophy. Influenced both Cicero and later Roman Stoics.

Learn more →
Ci

Cicero

106-43 BCE
Eclectic

Roman orator and philosopher who synthesized Greek philosophy for Roman audiences. Though not strictly Stoic, heavily influenced by Stoicism.

Late (Roman) Stoa

55 BCE-300 CE • Imperial Practice & Personal Ethics
Se

Seneca

4 BCE-65 CE
Statesman

Roman statesman, playwright, and Stoic philosopher. Advisor to Emperor Nero and one of the most influential Stoic writers.

Learn more →
M

Musonius Rufus

30-100 CE
Teacher

Known as the "Roman Socrates" for his emphasis on practical philosophy and moral character. Teacher of Epictetus.

Learn more →
Pu

Plutarch

46-120 CE
Critic

Platonist philosopher and biographer who critiqued Stoic philosophy, helping to clarify Stoic positions through criticism.

Learn more →
Ep

Epictetus

c. 50-135 CE
Teacher

Former slave who became one of the greatest Stoic teachers. His teachings focus on practical ethics and the dichotomy of control.

Learn more →
H

Hierocles

2nd century CE
Ethics

Stoic philosopher known for his theory of "circles of concern" and practical ethical teachings.

Learn more →
Sx

Sextus Empiricus

160-210 CE
Skeptic

Pyrrhonian skeptic who wrote detailed critiques of Stoic logic and epistemology, preserving many Stoic arguments.

Learn more →
MA

Marcus Aurelius

121-180 CE
Emperor

Roman Emperor and Stoic philosopher, one of the "Five Good Emperors." His personal journal became the "Meditations."

Learn more →
G

Galen

129-216 CE
Medical Critic

Physician and philosopher who critiqued Stoic psychology from a medical perspective, challenging Stoic theories.
