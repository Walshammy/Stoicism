I

Early Stoa

300-150 BCE • Foundation & Core Doctrine Development
S

Socrates

c. 470-399 BCE
Influence
The foundational figure whose teachings on virtue and the examined life profoundly influenced all subsequent philosophical schools, including Stoicism.
Z

Zeno of Citium

c. 335-263 BCE
Founder
Founder of Stoicism who established the school in Athens around 300 BCE. Combined Cynic ethics with academic philosophy to create systematic Stoic doctrine.
C

Cleanthes of Assos

c. 331-232 BCE
2nd Head
Second head of the Stoa who developed Stoic physics and theology. Known for his devotional approach to philosophy and poetic writings.
Ch

Chrysippus of Soli

c. 280-207 BCE
3rd Head
Third head of the Stoa, considered the "second founder" of Stoicism. Systematized Stoic doctrine and wrote over 700 works defending Stoic philosophy.
E

Epicurus

c. 341-270 BCE
Rival School
Founder of Epicureanism, the main philosophical rival to Stoicism. Advocated for pleasure as the highest good and atomistic materialism.
II

Middle Stoa

150-55 BCE • Adaptation & Roman Integration
D

Diogenes of Babylon

c. 240-152 BCE
Head of Stoa
Leading Stoic who helped introduce Stoicism to Rome through diplomatic missions. Emphasized practical ethics and rhetoric.
A

Antipater of Tarsus

2nd century BCE
Head of Stoa
Head of the Stoa who bridged Early and Middle Stoicism. Developed more nuanced views on emotions and practical wisdom.
P

Panaetius of Rhodes

c. 185-109 BCE
Head of Stoa
Leader of Middle Stoicism who adapted Stoic doctrine for Roman culture. Influenced Cicero and introduced more humanistic elements.
Po

Posidonius of Apamea

135-55 BCE
Stoic Polymath
Brilliant polymath who integrated scientific research with Stoic philosophy. Influenced both Cicero and later Roman Stoics.
Ci

Cicero

106-43 BCE
Academic/Eclectic
Roman orator and philosopher who synthesized Greek philosophy for Roman audiences. Though not strictly Stoic, heavily influenced by Stoicism.
III

Late (Roman) Stoa

55 BCE - 300 CE • Practical Ethics & Personal Development
Se

Seneca the Younger

c. 1-65 CE
Roman Stoic
Roman statesman, playwright, and Stoic philosopher. Advisor to Emperor Nero and one of the most influential Stoic writers.
M

Musonius Rufus

1st century CE
Roman Stoic
Known as the "Roman Socrates," he emphasized practical philosophy and was renowned for his moral character and teaching method.
Ep

Epictetus

c. 50-130 CE
Stoic Teacher
Former slave who became one of the greatest Stoic teachers. Focused on practical ethics and the discipline of desire.
MA

Marcus Aurelius

121-180 CE
Philosopher-Emperor
Roman Emperor and Stoic philosopher, known as one of the "Five Good Emperors." His personal journal became the "Meditations."
H

Hierocles

early 2nd century CE
Stoic Teacher
Stoic philosopher known for his theory of "circles of concern" and practical ethical teachings.
Pl

Plutarch

c. 50-120 CE
Critic
Platonist philosopher and biographer who wrote several works critiquing Stoic philosophy, particularly their ethics and physics.
SE

Sextus Empiricus

2nd century CE
Skeptic Critic
Pyrrhonian skeptic who wrote detailed critiques of Stoic logic and epistemology. His works preserve many Stoic arguments while attempting to refute them.
G

Galen

c. 130-201 CE
Medical Critic
Physician and philosopher who critiqued Stoic psychology and emotional theory from a medical perspective. His anatomical work challenged Stoic physics.
